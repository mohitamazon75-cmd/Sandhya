# Sandhya — Voice & Editorial Specification

**Status:** English register locked (Eknath Easwaran). Hindi register: founder review required.
**Purpose:** Every word that ships in Sandhya — daily readings, stories, principles, push messages, paywall copy, error messages, landing page — must obey this spec. The spec is the few-shot training data for the composer prompts and the editorial standard for human-written copy.

---

## 1. The English Register: Eknath Easwaran

### 1.1 What Easwaran's voice does

Easwaran's translations of the Gita, the Upanishads, and the Dhammapada are the gold standard for English Sanatan literature aimed at thoughtful adult readers. He is the model because he holds together five things most translators cannot:

1. **Plain language** — no Sanskrit jargon left untranslated, no academic distance.
2. **Quiet authority** — the writing is firm without being preachy.
3. **Warmth** — the reader feels addressed by someone who cares about them, not lectured.
4. **Pedagogical precision** — every word is doing work; nothing is filler.
5. **Refusal of mystification** — he never reaches for "energy," "vibrations," "the universe," or other Western-import vocabulary.

The composer prompt should aim to produce English that could plausibly appear in the introduction to one of his Penguin Classics translations.

### 1.2 Hard rules for the English register

**Verb tense and voice.**
- Active voice. Present tense.
- Second person ("today asks for restraint") not third person ("the day will bring restraint").

**Banned predictive verbs.** These are out, always:
- *will, going to, expect, foresee, brings, delivers, manifest, attract, reveal* (when used predictively about life events).

**Allowed orientation verbs.** These are in:
- *asks, inclines toward, emphasises, reminds, makes space for, calls for, points toward, holds, sits with, returns to.*

**Forbidden vocabulary** (zero exceptions):
- *energy, energies, vibration, vibrations, manifestation, manifest, attract, the universe is telling you, the cosmos, cosmic, frequencies, high vibe, low vibe, blessed, blessings rain down, abundance, prosperity mindset, alignment (in the new-age sense), spiritual journey, awakening, ascension, light worker, soul tribe, divine feminine / masculine* (when used as new-age tropes; legitimate references to Shakti and Shiva are fine in Sanskrit terms).

**Forbidden punctuation and formatting:**
- No exclamation marks. Ever.
- No emoji. Ever.
- No emphatic capitalization for spiritual words (do not write "the Self" with a capital S unless quoting a translation that does so; "the self" is correct).
- No bullet points inside daily readings, principles, or stories. Prose only.
- No bold for emphasis inside readings. The writing carries weight on its own.

**Sanskrit term policy:**
- Sanskrit terms are used directly when they have no clean English equivalent (*dharma*, *karma*, *moksha*, *samskara*, *vairagya*, *dasha*, *nakshatra*).
- They appear in roman script, not italicised in body copy. Italicising them is a translation-house affectation Easwaran avoids.
- They are glossed inline once per reading the first time they appear: "*samskara* — the imprint left by repeated action." After the first gloss, the term is used freely.
- Never English-jargon for Sanskrit concepts. "Self-actualisation" is not *moksha*. "Mindfulness" is not *dhyana*. Use the Sanskrit when the Sanskrit is what's meant.

**Length:**
- Daily reading: 80–120 words. Hard ceiling 130.
- Principle of the day: 80–120 words.
- Story: 200–400 words.
- Morning WhatsApp message body: under 60 words excluding link previews.
- Evening WhatsApp message: under 40 words.

**Address:**
- Use "you," not "the seeker" or "one." Direct.
- Do not use the user's name in every reading. Use it occasionally in the morning WhatsApp greeting, never inside the reading itself.

### 1.3 Five in-voice example daily readings

**Example 1 — A day asking for patience.**

> Today's chart asks for patience in places where you have been pushing. The Moon moves through Pushya, the *nakshatra* most associated in classical texts with nourishment and slow growth — not the kind of day that rewards force. Saturn aspects your tenth house, which adds weight to professional matters: what you do today gets watched, even if no one is watching.
>
> One practice: when you feel the urge to send a sharp message, write it as a draft and leave it in drafts until tomorrow. If it still seems necessary tomorrow, send it then. Most will not.

**Example 2 — A day after a hard one.**

> You may be tired. The chart does not pretend otherwise — yesterday's Mars-Saturn opposition does not vanish overnight, and the body remembers what the mind has moved past. Today is a recovery day, not a fresh-start day. Treat it that way.
>
> The teaching from the *Bhagavad Gita* most relevant to a day like this is in the second chapter: *yoga* is steadiness in success and steadiness in failure. The steadiness is the practice; the day's content is just weather. Walk slowly. Eat warm food. Speak less than usual.

**Example 3 — A day in a good window.**

> Today opens a small window. Jupiter transits your eleventh house, which classical texts associate with friendships, networks, and the fruits of past effort. This is not a day for big new beginnings — Jupiter does not push that way — but it is a day to send the message you have been delaying, to thank someone whose help you took for granted, to spend an hour with a friend without an agenda.
>
> One practice: name three people who shaped this year of your life. Tell one of them today.

**Example 4 — A day inside a hard dasha period.**

> You are in the third year of your Saturn *mahadasha*, and today's transit reinforces what these years are for. The classical reading is unsentimental: Saturn periods reward discipline and punish carelessness, and they do this regardless of how you feel about it. The work being asked is not glamorous. It is the work that makes everything afterward possible.
>
> Today's practice is the smallest version of this: choose one task you have been avoiding and do thirty minutes of it. Not the whole thing. Thirty minutes. The discipline is in beginning.

**Example 5 — A day with grief in it.**

> If you are grieving today, the chart does not minimise it. Ketu transits the fourth house — the seat of mother, home, and the inner ground. This is a configuration the tradition takes seriously; it is not a day for productivity advice.
>
> The teaching from the *Katha Upanishad* on a day like this is Yama's first answer to Nachiketa: the wise do not grieve. Yama does not mean grief is wrong. He means the loss is not what you think it is. Sit with that, slowly. Light a *diya* if you have one. Let the day be small.

### 1.4 Five out-of-voice readings (these would be rejected)

**Out-of-voice 1 — predictive language.**
> Today brings major opportunities for career growth! Jupiter is activating your tenth house, which means a promotion is coming. Be ready to seize the moment when it arrives.

*Wrong because:* date-stamped prediction, exclamation, future tense, "seize the moment" cliché, "activating" new-age vocabulary.

**Out-of-voice 2 — vague mysticism.**
> The cosmic energies today are aligning with your higher self. Trust the universe and let abundance flow into your life. Your intuition is your guide today.

*Wrong because:* "cosmic energies," "aligning," "higher self," "the universe," "abundance flow" — the entire forbidden-vocabulary set in one paragraph.

**Out-of-voice 3 — fear marketing.**
> Warning: Mars in your seventh house creates serious relationship dosha today. Avoid all important conversations. Tap here to book a remedy puja before things get worse.

*Wrong because:* manufactured fear, transaction tied to fear, "remedy" sold by us, urgency artificial.

**Out-of-voice 4 — over-soft Western-wellness register.**
> Hi beautiful soul ✨ Today is all about self-care and gentle vibes. Take a moment to honour yourself and breathe into your sacred space. You deserve all the love.

*Wrong because:* "beautiful soul," emoji, "vibes," "sacred space," "you deserve all the love" — saccharine, emoji-using, infantilising.

**Out-of-voice 5 — over-academic.**
> The current planetary configuration evidences a Mars-Saturn yoga in the kendra, which according to Brihat Parashara Hora Shastra (chapter 36, verse 12) indicates *karma-bhanga* tendencies. Practitioners of *jyotish* will recognise this as a *vipareeta-rajayoga* if other conditions obtain.

*Wrong because:* unglossed Sanskrit, citation-heavy, written for astrologers not for the user, no practice offered, no warmth.

## 2. The Hindi Register

**Status: DRAFT — founder review required before locking.** I (Claude) am noting that my training on literary Hindi is weaker than English, so this section is provisional and we refine it together in the next session.

### 2.1 The aim

The Hindi register should feel like a warm, well-read elder uncle or aunt — formal *aap*, never *tum* — who happens to write clearly. The mental model is *Premchand's prose register* applied to short-form spiritual writing: dignified, plain, free of both Sanskritic stiffness and Anglicised vocabulary. Not Hinglish — Hinglish is its own register and we handle it separately.

### 2.2 Provisional rules

- *Aap* throughout. Never *tum*.
- Sanskrit words are used directly (दर्शन, दशा, नक्षत्र, धर्म, कर्म) without italicisation. They are part of Hindi already and need no defending.
- Avoid hyper-Sanskritised Hindi (अद्यैव, परम्, etc.) — this drifts toward textbook stiffness.
- Avoid English loan-words for spiritual concepts. "Energy" "vibe" "manifest" "journey" all banned in Hindi too. Use the Hindi/Sanskrit equivalents.
- Punctuation: same rules as English. No exclamation marks. No emoji.
- Length: Hindi text is generally 10–15% denser than English. Daily reading in Hindi: 70–100 शब्द.

### 2.4 The Hinglish register

For users who choose Hinglish (Roman script), the register is closer to *educated urban Indian English* with Hindi vocabulary used naturally, not performatively. "Aaj ka din thoda patience maangta hai" is fine. "Today the universe is sending you good vibes, bhai" is not. Hinglish is a real register, not a gimmick — write it the way a thoughtful 32-year-old in Mumbai actually writes to a friend, not the way an ad agency writes for one.

## 3. On Fear and Tough Love

This is the most important rule in the spec. Get this wrong in either direction and the product breaks.

### 3.1 The principle

Sanatan Dharma does not sanitise fear. Yama teaches Nachiketa through fear of death. The Gita opens with Arjuna's terror and Krishna does not soothe it — he sharpens it into action. Bhakti literature is full of warnings, narakas, and Yama's messengers. The Mahamrityunjaya exists because the tradition takes the reality of death seriously enough to address it directly. A Sandhya that never names a hard truth, never warns, never carries weight — that Sandhya is dishonest to the tradition and weak medicine.

But the tradition's fear is always *in service of the listener's clarity.* It is never engineered to drive a transaction. When a guru warns, the warning is the guru's gift; there is no payment associated with the warning being lifted. This is the line.

### 3.2 The structural test

Before writing or approving any content that invokes fear, gravity, warning, or hard truth, run this test:

> Would I write this same warning if Sandhya had no paid tier at all?

- If yes: the fear is honest. Ship it.
- If no — if I'm reaching for the warning specifically to drive conversion: it's a dark pattern. Don't ship it.

### 3.3 Fear allowed (honest tough love)

- The classical reading of a Saturn period is unsentimental, and the user is in one — say so.
- Today's transit historically warns against impulsive financial decisions — say so. Don't bury it under reassurance.
- The user is in the closing year of a major dasha and the next is materially different — name the gravity. The tradition's framing of life-time *is* a kind of weight the user benefits from feeling.
- The Mahabharata stories are not all happy. Yudhishthira lies once and goes to hell briefly. Karna's life is a tragedy. We tell those stories whole, not edited.
- The principle of the day can be hard: *anitya* (impermanence), *mrityu-bhavana* (contemplation of death — a real classical practice), the reality of karmic consequence.
- Evening reflection prompts can be sharp: "where did fear move you today, and where did clarity?" "What did you avoid that the day asked of you?"

### 3.4 Fear banned (manufactured for transaction)

- "You have severe Mangal dosha — book a remedy puja before disaster strikes." (Manufactured urgency tied to product we sell.)
- "Sade Sati is destroying your life — consult an astrologer now." (Sensationalised; transaction-tied.)
- "Only 3 muhurta windows left this month! Click to book." (Fake scarcity.)
- Push notifications using fear language to drive opens.
- Paywall copy that uses spiritual anxiety to drive subscription.

### 3.5 The handling of real classical "doshas" and harsh configurations

When a user actually has Mangal dosha, Sade Sati, Kaal Sarp, weak marakas, etc. — the tradition has things to say about these, and the user deserves to hear what the tradition says. Sandhya handles them as follows:

- **Discussed honestly** when the day's chart actually involves the configuration. Classical interpretation, no minimising, no sensationalising.
- **Never blasted as the headline.** The user is more than their dosha; daily reading leads with the day's emphasis, not the lifelong configuration.
- **Practices offered are user-action practices** (mantras, contemplations, behavioural disciplines). Never "buy this remedy from us."
- **The tone is the tradition's own tone**, which for these heavy configurations is grave but not panicked. The user feels addressed seriously, not scared into purchase.

## 4. Voice Across Surfaces

### 4.1 WhatsApp morning message
Brief, structured, warm. Use the user's name in the greeting only. The structured fields (auspicious window, colour, mantra, do, don't) are short — phrases, not sentences. The one-line summary at the top is in voice. Total under 60 words.

### 4.2 WhatsApp evening message
Even briefer. One reflection prompt + sleep mantra link. Under 40 words.

### 4.3 Daily reading on web
Full voice spec applies. 80–120 words.

### 4.4 Story rendering
Voice-rewritten from canonical source. 200–400 words. No stated moral at the end — the story lands.

### 4.5 Principle rendering
80–120 words. Source attribution always present (text name + verse reference, when available). The principle is stated, illustrated briefly, and tied back to lived experience.

### 4.6 Landing page copy
Same voice. No exclamation marks. No "Discover your destiny!" No "Unlock your karma!" Methodology section explains what we actually do in plain language. Founder voice is honest about being a small product made carefully.

### 4.7 Paywall copy
"Continue reading" not "Unlock the full experience!" The paywall is informational, not seductive. Price is clearly stated. Cancel-anytime is clearly stated. No countdown timers, no fake discounts, no "limited spots."

### 4.8 Error messages and edge cases
Plain, kind, never falsely cheerful. "Something went wrong loading today's reading. We're looking at it. Please try again in a few minutes." Not "Oops! 🙏 Looks like the cosmos had other plans!"

### 4.9 Push notifications (none in v1 except the morning + evening WhatsApp)
If we ever add push notifications in v2, they obey the same voice. No exclamation marks. No fear language. No FOMO.

## 5. The Editorial Veto

When in doubt, the founder has the editorial veto over any content that ships. The composer prompt produces drafts; the founder approves before content enters the production pool. This is non-negotiable for the first 6 months of operation. After 6 months, if a clear pattern of approval has emerged, the founder may delegate routine approval to a hired editor — but the voice spec remains the standard.

If the composer ever produces output that violates a hard rule in this document, that's a bug in the prompt, not a stylistic choice. The prompt is updated with examples of the violation as a negative case, and the spec is followed.
